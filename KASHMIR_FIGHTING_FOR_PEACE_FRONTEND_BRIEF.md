## PART ONE — THE FILM
### What It Is. What It Does. Why It Exists.

---

**Kashmir — Fighting for Peace** is a feature-length documentary by Rig 360 Media. It is approximately 70 minutes of journalism conducted on the ground in Kashmir — not from a studio, not from a distance, but from inside the conflict itself. The journalist is present. The camera is present. The people are present.

It is not a political film. It does not argue for India or Pakistan. It does not glorify soldiers or demonize militants. It does not simplify.

What it does is this: it places the camera in front of ordinary human beings — fathers, daughters, officers, militants, grieving mothers, young girls on bicycles — and it asks them to speak. And then it listens. And what emerges from that listening is something that no news headline, no government statement, no political speech has ever captured about Kashmir: **the full, unresolved, unbearable human cost of a conflict that has been running for decades and has learned to consume everything it touches — beauty, faith, family, youth, and peace itself.**

The title contains the film's central contradiction: *Fighting for Peace.* You cannot fight for peace without the fighting itself becoming the wound. Every person in this film is doing exactly that — fighting, in their own way, for some version of peace — and every person in this film is paying a price for it that no ideology can justify and no victory can repay.

**The website must carry this contradiction in its bones.** Not as a tagline. As a lived reality that the user feels from the first second they arrive to the last second they leave.

## PART TWO — THE FILM, SCENE BY SCENE
### The Complete Narrative, In Order, With Full Emotional Context

---

Read this section the way you would watch the film — in sequence, without skipping. The emotional register of each scene is as important as its content. Design implications are noted inline where they are most relevant.

---

### SCENE 1 — NIGHT. SOLDIERS. THE WORLD BEFORE DAWN.

The film opens in darkness.

Not metaphorical darkness — literal night in Kashmir. Soldiers are on patrol. They move through streets that are empty, quiet, and tense in the way that only a place with a long memory of violence can be quiet and tense at the same time.

They communicate on walkie-talkies. The language they use is military — codes, designations, numbers, directional terms. "Delta." Position numbers. Weapon references — MGL. This is coordinated movement, professional and practiced. These are not nervous men. They are doing something they have done many times.

Then something happens. A confrontation. The reason is not elaborated — it doesn't need to be. Soldiers start firing. The scene is real. This is not staged. The camera captures it as it happens.

And then — it cuts.

**What this opening does:** It drops the viewer into the reality of Kashmir without introduction, without context, without preparation. There is no narrator yet, no establishing shot of mountains and beauty. The first thing you see is soldiers in the dark and then gunfire. The film is saying: *this is what is real here. Now let us show you everything else that is also real.*

**Design implication — the Hero section:** The website's opening must mirror this energy. Not with gunfire — but with the same quality of dropping someone into something real without warning. Darkness first. Then the title emerging. No cheerful animation. No welcoming UI. The site opens the way the film opens — you are already inside before you've decided to enter.

---

### SCENE 2 — MORNING. KASHMIR. THE BEAUTY THAT MAKES THE DARKNESS WORSE.

The night cuts to morning.

And Kashmir in the morning is — there is no other word for it — breathtaking. The film takes its time here. Mountains. Light. The particular quality of air and landscape that makes Kashmir one of the most visually extraordinary places on earth.

Then the narration begins. And what it describes is not the conflict — not yet. It describes Kashmir's artisans. Its craftsmen. The people who have, for generations, made things of extraordinary beauty with their hands — woodwork, carpets, shawls, objects that carry in their making a kind of cultural memory and pride. The narration describes what these objects mean. What the skill means. What it says about the people who live here.

This is deliberate. The film is establishing something before it shows you the darkness: *these are not a people defined by conflict. They are a people with a civilization, a culture, an artistry that existed long before this and will exist long after.* The conflict is something that happened to them. It is not what they are.

Then military vehicles begin rolling through those same streets. The beauty and the machinery occupy the same frame. Same roads. Same Kashmir.

**What this scene does:** It establishes the film's central visual and emotional duality — extraordinary beauty and the presence of conflict coexisting in the same space, neither canceling the other out. This duality will run through the entire film and must run through the entire website.

**Design implication — The Duality:** This is the origin of the site's signature element. The split experience of Kashmir's beauty and Kashmir's reality is not a design choice — it is the film's thesis stated visually in Scene 2. Every parallax image, every beauty-beside-grief layout choice, every moment where a stunning landscape sits next to a devastating quote — it all comes from this scene.

---

### SCENE 3 — THE ARSENAL. JOURNALISM AS WITNESS.

The morning beauty gives way to a different kind of scene.

A military camp. A display of weapons — arsenals laid out, visible, documented. These are confiscated weapons. They belonged to a terrorist who was caught. The military is showing them to journalists. An officer describes what each weapon is, where it came from, what it means that it was here.

The weapons are from Pakistan.

This scene is journalism in its most direct form — an official display of evidence, on the record, for the camera. It is not dramatic. It is not staged for effect. It is the military saying: *here is what we found. Here is what was coming across that border. Look at it.*

**What this scene does:** It grounds the film in documented, physical reality before the interviews begin. There is no ambiguity about the existence of cross-border militant activity. The weapons are there. They are real. This is not allegation — it is inventory.

---

### SCENE 4 — THE INTERVIEW BEGINS. THE CAPTURED TERRORIST.

Now the film introduces its most complex and ultimately most humanizing subject: a young man who crossed from Pakistan into India as a member of a militant group. He was caught. He is being held. And now he is sitting across from the journalist, one on one, answering questions.

This interview will return multiple times throughout the film — it is one of the structural anchors of the documentary. Each time we return to it, we go deeper. But the first time we see him, the journalist is establishing the basics: who is he, what did he do, what was his mindset.

He is young. He is not a monster. He is a young man who was told certain things, believed them, acted on them, and is now sitting in custody in a country he was told was the enemy, talking to a journalist.

**What this scene does:** It refuses the easy narrative. A captured militant could be shown as a villain — dangerous, unrepentant, defined by his actions. Instead the film sits with him and asks him to be a person. This is a choice that defines the moral character of the entire documentary.

**Design implication — The Voices section:** Every interview subject on the website must be presented with this same refusal of easy categorization. The captured militant gets the same visual treatment as the grieving father, the same as the army officer. Dark, dignified, human. No one is a symbol. Everyone is a person.

---

### SCENE 5 — THE ARMY POST. THE OFFICER'S TRUTH.

An army post. The film shows its setting — the physical reality of where these soldiers live and work. Their positions. Their equipment. The landscape they are responsible for.

An officer speaks. He describes the working conditions. The hardships. What it means to do this duty, in this place, with this level of responsibility. He is not complaining — he is accounting. Describing reality as it is.

The journalist asks him about infiltration. How does it happen? How do people cross the LOC — the Line of Control — from Pakistan into India?

The officer explains. The border is long. The terrain is difficult. And critically — night is when it happens. Darkness is the infiltrator's cover. The same darkness the film opened in.

**What this scene does:** It connects the opening night patrol to the larger reality of what those soldiers are doing and why. The darkness of Scene 1 now has full context.

---

### SCENE 6 — HOW THE TERRORIST WAS CAUGHT. HOW THE OTHERS DIED.

The narration here describes the night operation that led to the captured terrorist's arrest.

His group crossed. They were found. There was a confrontation.

He surrendered. The others did not. The others were killed.

This is stated plainly. Those who surrendered lived. Those who didn't, died. The captured terrorist we are interviewing is alive because he made a choice in that moment — a choice that the film does not judge, simply notes.

**What this scene does:** It gives the captured terrorist's survival its full weight. He is alive because he chose differently than the men beside him in that moment. That choice — and what it has led to, including this interview — is what the film is examining.

---

### SCENE 7 — BACK TO THE INTERVIEW. THE FULL JOURNEY.

The film returns to the captured terrorist. Now the journalist goes deeper — not just what happened the night he was caught, but everything before it. His full story.

How he became who he became. The journey from ordinary young man to someone who crossed an international border with weapons. The process of radicalization, recruitment, training. What he was told. What he believed. What he was promised. What he thought he was doing.

He answers. He is not evasive. He is — and this is what makes these interview sequences so powerful — honest in a way that is almost disarming. He describes his journey as he experienced it, which means he describes the ideology from the inside, the way it felt true when it was being built in him.

**What this scene does:** It gives the film its most important structural element — a first-person account of radicalization from someone who lived it and is now, in custody, able to look back at it. This is rare. This is journalism earning its place.

---

### SCENE 8 — THE HISTORY OF KASHMIR. AND THEN A FUNERAL.

Narration over Kashmir footage. The film steps back and provides historical context — the origins of the Kashmir conflict, the political and territorial history that created the conditions for everything we are watching.

This is the film locating the human stories within the larger story. It is not a lecture — it is context, briefly provided, so that what follows lands with its full weight.

And then: a police officer has been shot and killed in a confrontation with militants.

The film shows his funeral.

This is one of the most visually and emotionally significant sequences in the documentary. A ceremony. People in grief. The rituals of loss being performed for a man who died doing his job. The formality of a funeral — the uniform, the ceremony, the finality — against the faces of the people who loved him.

Then the family speaks. His father. The impact of his loss described not as a statistic but as the destruction of a specific future, a specific person, a specific set of relationships. Who he was. What he wanted. What will not happen now.

**What this scene does:** It makes the cost of the conflict personal and specific. Not "security forces have been killed." This man. This family. This funeral. This father.

**Design implication:** This is why the website cannot use generic "conflict" imagery or stock photography. Every visual choice must feel like it is honoring a specific human being, not illustrating a news story.

---

### SCENE 9 — THE IPS OFFICER. THE PROXY WAR EXPLAINED.

A senior police officer — likely IPS rank — gives the film its clearest analytical framework for what is happening in Kashmir.

He explains Pakistan's proxy war strategy. He explains how it works: not by sending armies across borders (though that happens too, as the weapons display showed) but by finding people who already live on the Indian side. Young Kashmiri men. Boys, sometimes. People who have grievances, who are vulnerable, who can be contacted, manipulated, and gradually converted into instruments of violence against their own place and their own people.

He describes the central difficulty of this kind of conflict: the enemy is not a foreign army you can see coming. The enemy is someone who grew up here, who looks like everyone else, who is someone's son. You cannot find this enemy with a satellite. You find him — sometimes too late — in the aftermath of what he has done.

**What this scene does:** It explains the human tragedy of the father interviews that follow. These fathers did not raise militants. They raised sons. Something happened between who their sons were and who their sons became — and that something is what the IPS officer is describing.

---

### SCENE 10 — THE FATHERS. THE MOST PAINFUL INTERVIEWS IN THE FILM.

Now the film delivers its most devastating material.

Two fathers. Interviewed separately. Each having lost a son to militancy — meaning their sons are alive, or were alive, but are gone. Chosen a path that took them away from their families and into violence.

**Father One:** His son is accused of involvement in a militant attack. He sits with this. The accusation against his child. What it means to be the father of someone accused of this.

**Father Two:** He describes the last contact he had with his son. One phone call. In the past year. His son called and told him: I cannot come back. There are circumstances. There are people around me. There are compulsions I cannot explain. I may want to return — and the father holds onto this, the possibility that his son wants to come home — but something is holding him back.

That was the last call. One year of silence since.

The father describes what this has done to his family. To his wife — the boy's mother. She is not managing. She is not "coping" in any functional sense. She is a woman who had a son she loved, who got one phone call telling her he cannot come home, and who is now waiting in a silence that has no end date and no resolution.

He describes her condition. She keeps asking for her son. Every day. Just asking where he is, when he is coming back. The question that has no answer. Asked every day.

**What this scene does:** It locates the human cost of radicalization not in the militant himself but in the people left behind by his choice. These parents did not make this choice. They are living inside its consequences without having had any say in it. The mother asking for her son every day is the film's quietest and most unbearable image — and it is not even an image, it is a description. A father describing his wife asking for their son. Every day.

**Design implication — This is the emotional center of the film.** Not the gunfire. Not the dead bodies. This. A mother asking for her son and no answer coming. When the website has a moment of maximum emotional weight — a pull quote, a section header, a closing element — this is the register it must reach for.

---

### SCENE 11 — BACK TO THE OFFICER. BACK TO THE FATHERS.

The film returns briefly to the police officer interview. He elaborates on the mechanism — how Pakistani handlers make contact with young Kashmiri men, how the process of manipulation works, how gradually someone who had no intention of becoming a militant is converted into one.

Then another father. Brief. Another dimension of the same loss.

Then back to Father Two — the man with the last phone call. The journalist asks: when your son told you he had chosen this path, what was happening with him? What was the situation?

He answers. He tries to understand, even now, what led his son there. What the circumstances were. What the compulsions were that his son mentioned on that last call. He is not angry at his son in this moment — he is trying, from a distance of one year of silence, to understand.

**What this scene does:** It shows a father still trying to love his son even after everything. Still trying to make sense of it. That refusal to simply condemn is one of the most human things in the film.

---

### SCENE 12 — MILITARY VEHICLES. THE NARRATION CONTINUES.

Military convoys moving through Kashmir. The landscape around them. The narration continues about the father interviews, about what they reveal about the conflict.

This is the film using its visual language — the movement of military vehicles through beautiful terrain — as a kind of ambient truth. The conflict is always moving. It does not stop between interviews.

---

### SCENE 13 — THE CONFRONTATION. RAW FOOTAGE. DEAD BODIES.

The film shows a military confrontation. Soldiers firing. Militants.

Then: the aftermath. Bodies on the ground, seen from above. A top-down view. The way you see the dead when you are standing over them.

This is real footage. Not reconstruction. Not dramatization. The film does not dwell on it, does not slow-motion it, does not score it for emotional effect. It shows it the way it happened — quickly, clinically, finally.

**What this scene does:** It reminds the viewer — in case the interview sequences had created any distance — that this conflict kills people. That the militants who do not surrender end up like this. That the soldiers who get shot end up in the funeral we saw earlier.

**Design implication:** The website should never aestheticize violence. No weapon iconography as decoration. No blood-red color used for visual flair. The Dull Crimson in the palette exists to be used sparingly, with intention, where loss is being acknowledged — not as branding.

---

### SCENE 14 — THE OFFICER WHO COUNTS SAVED, NOT KILLED.

An army officer is interviewed. The journalist asks him about the militancy problem — specifically the reality that many of the young people being radicalized are Kashmiri, meaning the army is, in some sense, fighting against the people it is also protecting.

The officer's answer reframes everything.

He says his measure of success is not how many militants are killed. It is how many young people are prevented from becoming militants. How many are saved before they reach the point of no return.

He is a soldier saying: I do not want to kill. I want to save. My job, as I understand it, is not to neutralize threats — it is to stop threats from forming. To reach the young man before the handler does. To offer something — respect, a future, dignity — that the militant recruiter is also offering, but at a price this officer is not willing to let these boys pay.

**What this scene does:** It provides the film's moral argument for what the Indian security presence in Kashmir should look like at its best. It is also a quiet indictment of every approach that prioritizes body counts over human lives saved.

**Design implication:** This quote — or the sentiment it contains — belongs somewhere prominent on the site. Not as propaganda. As the film's statement of what it hopes for.

---

### SCENE 15 — THE CONDITIONS THAT CREATE MILITANTS.

The police officer interview continues — or a different officer elaborates — on the conditions that make young boys vulnerable. What their lives look like. What they are offered. What they are not offered. The gap between what they have and what they are told they deserve that the militant recruiter exploits.

Behind this interview, visually: soldiers and police preparing to leave for duty. The rhythms of daily operational life in Kashmir. People doing their jobs.

**What this scene does:** It humanizes the process of radicalization without excusing it. It says: these are not boys who were born wanting to become militants. They were made. Understanding how they were made is not sympathy — it is strategy. You cannot stop a process you do not understand.

---

### SCENE 16 — THE SURRENDER. THE MOST COMPLEX SCENE IN THE FILM.

Police have located a group of militants. Young men. The police move to confront them.

But instead of a firefight, something else happens.

The police call out to them. Come out. Surrender. And then — the parents are brought. The actual parents of these young men. And the parents call out to their children. In their own local language. The language of home. The language their children learned before they learned anything else.

Come out. We are here. Surrender. Come home.

After time — after the weight of hearing their parents' voices, in that language, from that distance — the militants come out. They surrender.

**What this scene does:** It is almost impossible to fully describe what this scene contains.

Think about every element simultaneously: parents who have been living with the knowledge that their child is out there armed and wanted. Police who chose to bring those parents rather than simply open fire. Young men who, when they heard the voices of the people who loved them first and longest, chose to live rather than die for an ideology. A language that carries home in its sound, used to cross the distance between a son and the people he left behind.

It worked. That is the most stunning thing. It worked.

This is the film's single moment of something approaching grace. Not redemption — the surrender does not undo what was done. Not resolution — the conflict continues. But grace: a situation that could have ended in more death ended instead in life. Because someone chose to bring the parents. Because the parents came. Because the sons heard them.

**Design implication:** If the website has one moment of visual and emotional lightness — one place where the darkness lifts even slightly — it should be here. Not cheerful. Not triumphant. Just: the possibility that human connection can sometimes reach further than ideology. That a parent's voice in a mother tongue can do what a rifle cannot.

---

### SCENE 17 — THE GIRLS ON CYCLES. THE OTHER KASHMIR.

After the surrender, the film shows something that feels almost startling in context: a group of young Kashmiri girls doing a cycle rally.

They are asked about their lives. They speak simply and directly: they want what young people everywhere want. A normal life. To do the things that young people in other cities, other places, do. To have their youth be youth — not a political situation, not a conflict zone, not a dateline in someone else's news story.

They are not angry. They are not traumatized on screen. They are just young women who want to ride bikes and have a normal life and who, by their simple existence and their simple desire, represent something the rest of the film has been unable to show: the possibility of ordinary.

**What this scene does:** It is the film's breath. Its single sustained moment of something that is not grief. These girls did not lose anyone in this scene. They are not recounting a tragedy. They are just here, wanting what they want, existing in the same Kashmir that everything else in this film has shown bleeding.

**Design implication:** The website must have one element that carries this energy. Not hope in a grand sense. Just the stubborn, quiet, beautiful fact of ordinary life continuing. Young women on bicycles. That simple. That important.

---

### SCENE 18 — BACK TO THE FUNERAL. BACK TO THE FATHER OF THE FALLEN OFFICER.

The film returns to the police officer who was killed. His funeral ceremony footage plays. Then his father is interviewed.

His father describes what it is to lose a son who died in service. The particular grief of a loss that has a flag on the coffin — that is honored publicly, that is called sacrifice — but that is, underneath all of that, simply the loss of his child. His son. The person he raised.

The ceremony is formal. The grief is not.

**What this scene does:** It completes a portrait that was begun in Scene 8. We have now seen the funeral, heard from the family, heard the father's grief. The officer who was killed is no longer an abstraction — he is a son, and his father is sitting here telling us what it is like to be the father of someone who died this way.

---

### SCENE 19 — WINTER. THE NARRATION ABOUT WHAT THE SNOW COVERS.

The film's narration shifts to Kashmir's winters. The visual beauty of snow — the white that covers everything, that makes Kashmir look like something out of a dream or a fairytale.

But the narration holds the darkness alongside the beauty. Under the snow, the same reality. Families who lost someone fighting militants. Families who lost someone to militant groups. The snow does not resolve anything. It just makes it quiet for a while.

**What this scene does:** It is the film returning to its central duality — beauty and grief, the same place, inseparable. It is also the film acknowledging that both kinds of loss — the soldier's family and the militant's family — are real loss. Both are grief. The snow does not distinguish.

---

### SCENE 20 — CONSECUTIVE GRIEF INTERVIEWS.

Several interviews, one after another. Different families. Different losses.

Some lost someone who was protecting Kashmir — a soldier, a police officer, someone in service. Some lost someone who was taken by militancy — a son who joined, a child who was recruited and lost to the ideology.

The film places these interviews in sequence without editorial comment. It is saying: look at how many families. Look at how many losses. Look at how the grief on this side looks like the grief on that side. They are not the same loss — but they are the same grief.

**Design implication:** The "Voices" section of the website must preserve this — the equal weight given to every interview subject regardless of which "side" their loss falls on. The visual treatment must be identical. The dignified darkness of a portrait, the weight of a quote, applied to everyone.

---

### SCENE 21 — STONE PELTING. THE POLICE OFFICER'S TRAPPED DAY.

The film shows stone pelting — the particular form of protest-violence that has been part of Kashmir's conflict for years. Young people throwing stones at security forces. The forces trying to manage it.

Then the police officer takes the journalist somewhere specific. A location.

He was here once. During a protest. A mob formed around this place, and the police officer was trapped inside — locked in by the crowd outside. Stone pelting. Confrontation. The mob holding the building.

He describes that day in detail. The sounds of it. The experience of being an officer of the law, trapped inside a building, by the people of the place he is supposed to serve. The particular impossibility of that situation — not an enemy you can respond to with force, because these are the people. But not safety either.

He stayed. The situation resolved somehow. He is here now, describing it.

**What this scene does:** It captures the impossible position of the local security force with granular, specific, experiential detail. This is not a policy discussion. This is a man describing a specific afternoon when he was trapped in a building by a mob. The conflict made personal through one day, one location, one officer's memory.

---

### SCENE 22 — DAILY LIFE IN KASHMIR. THE STREETS. THE ORDINARY.

Between the heavier interview sequences, the film shows Kashmir's streets. Daily life. People going about their business. The ordinary rhythms of a place that is also a conflict zone — both of those things true at the same time.

The narration continues about militancy, about the situation. But visually: just people. Just streets. Just Kashmir being Kashmir.

**What this scene does:** It maintains the film's insistence that Kashmir is a place with a life, not just a conflict. The people in the streets are not extras in someone else's political drama. They are living here.

---

### SCENE 23 — THE SIKH OFFICER. TARGETED. HIS DAUGHTER.

A Sikh police officer was killed. Not in a confrontation with armed militants. He was beaten to death.

And the specific detail that makes this killing different from the others in the film: he was singled out. There were five or six buses. The mob targeted his bus specifically. Because he was Sikh. Because of what he looked like, what his faith was, how he could be identified and separated from the others.

His daughter is interviewed. She describes the incident — what she was told, what she understood, what it meant to learn that her father was killed not for anything he did but for what he was. That someone looked at the buses, looked for him specifically, and chose him.

She describes the impact. Her father. What she lost. What it means to carry the knowledge of how he died.

**What this scene does:** It adds a communal, targeted dimension to the violence in the film that is distinct from everything else. The other deaths in the film are, in some sense, occupational — soldiers and officers dying in confrontations that are part of the conflict. This death was a choice made about identity. It was personal in a way that is specifically terrible.

**Design implication:** This moment must be handled with complete gravity on the website. No visual language that aestheticizes it. Just the weight of what happened and who was left behind.

---

### SCENE 24 — RELIGION WEAPONIZED. THE OFFICER'S RESPONSE.

The journalist asks the police officer directly about the "Islamic war" narrative — the framing, used by some militant groups and their supporters, that the conflict in Kashmir is a religious war for Islam. A jihad.

The officer's response is clear and direct: this is not what Islam is. This is not what Islam teaches. This violence is not for the cause of Islam — it is against the cause of Islam. Islam is a religion of peace and love. It does not instruct people to kill. What is being done in its name here is a corruption of it, not an expression of it.

He is a Muslim officer saying this. It is not an outsider's critique. It is someone from within the faith refusing to allow the faith to be defined by what is being done in its name.

**What this scene does:** It is the film's direct engagement with the ideological framing that militant recruiters use. It refuses it — not politically, but religiously and morally — from a position of faith.

---

### SCENE 25 — THE TERRORIST'S IDEOLOGY COLLAPSES.

The film returns to the captured Pakistani terrorist. Now the journalist asks him about the specific claims he was told before crossing the border — that Muslims in India are oppressed, that the Indian government forbids them from practicing their religion, that Kashmiri Muslims are suffering under a regime that hates them.

He was told these things. He believed them. He crossed based on them.

And then he describes what he found.

The officers around him in custody. The soldiers he encountered. Many of them Muslim. Praying. Practicing their faith. Serving India. Defending the Indian state.

His entire ideological foundation collapsed in the encounter with reality. He had come to liberate people who did not consider themselves imprisoned. He had come to protect Muslims who were the very people holding him. The story he had been told — the story that justified everything he did — was false. Not partly false. Structurally, foundationally false.

He also came to understand something else: there are some people in Kashmir who want to be part of Pakistan. But there are many, many more Muslims in India who are praying, practicing, living, serving — and the image of Muslim oppression that was used to recruit him had nothing to do with the reality he found.

**What this scene does:** It is the film's most quietly devastating intellectual moment. A young man discovering that he was a tool. That the people who built his ideology knew what he would find on the other side of that border and sent him anyway. That his sacrifice — and the deaths of the men beside him who didn't surrender — was in service of a lie.

He is not enraged on screen. He is something quieter than rage. He is a person who has had to reconstruct reality after the framework he had been given turned out to be false.

---

### SCENE 26 — THE MILITARY OFFICER. THE REAL CHALLENGE.

A military officer is interviewed about terrorism and its future in Kashmir. He makes a distinction that the film treats as one of its key analytical insights:

The military challenge — neutralizing active militants, securing the LOC, conducting operations — is manageable. The Indian security forces have the competency, training, and resources to deal with armed threats.

The real challenge is the ecosystem around the threat. The system by which young Kashmiri men are identified, contacted, manipulated, and converted. The economy that has grown up around the conflict — people who profit from violence continuing, from instability persisting, from the situation never resolving. And connected to all of this: drugs.

He explains the drug-terror connection. Drug money funds militant operations. Drug addiction is used as a tool — to compromise young people, to control them, to make them dependent on the same networks that then use them for violence. The same pipelines that move weapons move narcotics. The economy of terror and the economy of addiction are the same economy.

This is not abstract. This is a system with specific victims and specific beneficiaries.

**What this scene does:** It gives the film's analysis of the conflict its most complete form. The bullets are not the problem — the bullets are the symptom. The problem is the infrastructure that produces the bullets, funds them, and ensures there are always more young men willing to carry them.

---

### SCENE 27 — THE BROTHER. AND THE TWO MOST DEVASTATING FATHER INTERVIEWS.

Three interviews in sequence. Each one harder than the last.

**The Brother:** His sibling was both a drug addict and a militant. These two things happened together — the addiction made him controllable, the militancy gave that control a purpose. The brother describes this. What he watched happen to his sibling.

**Father Three:** His son joined a militant group. He has arrived at a place of acceptance so broken it barely resembles acceptance. He says: if his son is found, and if killing him is what is required for the rest of the family to survive — to live a normal life, to not be suspended in this half-existence of waiting and shame and surveillance — then so be it.

He is angry. Furiously, quietly, devastatingly angry. His son betrayed him. Betrayed the family. Chose an ideology over the people who loved him. What did the religion give him that the family couldn't? What was so important? The father cannot answer these questions. He just sits with the anger and the love and the acceptance of the worst outcome simultaneously.

**Father Four:** His son also joined. But the specific detail here is the mother. His son was her favorite child. Deeply, visibly, known-by-everyone favorite. And the son loved his mother back with equal depth — that bond was real and witnessed and part of who both of them were.

Now his wife has had a mental breakdown. She sits somewhere in the house. She keeps asking for her son. Every day. Just asking. Where is he. When is he coming back.

The father describes this. He is watching his wife disappear into grief for a child who is alive but unreachable. The son chose ideology over a mother who loved him more than anyone. The mother cannot process this choice. It is outside the boundaries of what she can understand. So her mind has found a way around understanding it: she just keeps asking. As if the answer might come.

**What this scene does:** These three interviews together are the film's most sustained engagement with grief. By this point in the documentary the viewer has heard many stories of loss. But something about these three — the brother, the father who accepted his son's death, the father whose wife keeps asking — is different. It is not louder. It is quieter. It is grief that has run out of energy for drama and is just sitting there, living in the body of these men, every day.

**Design implication:** If there is one cluster of moments the entire website must be built to honor — this is it. The father who accepted his son's death. The mother who keeps asking. These are the people the website exists for. Every design choice, every animation, every quote — should be worthy of sitting in the same space as these men and what they carry.

---

### SCENE 28 — THE CORDON. THE OPERATION BEGINNING AGAIN.

An army officer describes how they secure an area for an operation. The methodology — how teams are positioned, how the cordon is established, how coordination works between units. Professional, practiced, precise.

A group of soldiers prepares to leave for night duty. They are getting ready. Checking equipment. Moving out.

We have seen this before — or rather, we are about to see where this leads. The film is circling back to where it began.

---

### SCENE 29 — THE TERRORIST AND HIS MOTHER.

Near the end of his interview arc, the journalist asks the captured Pakistani terrorist about his mother.

Does she know? Does she know what happened — that he crossed a border, that he fought, that he was caught and is being held?

He says: no. He has not told her.

The journalist asks if he sends her money. He says he does what he can.

Then the conversation moves to ideology — to jihad, to the justification for what he did. And he does something striking: he quotes the Quran. He says that the Quran teaches that one should serve one's parents first. That this is the primary obligation.

And then, in almost the same breath, he explains why he still crossed the border. Why protecting Muslim dignity — as he understood it — was also necessary. He holds both things: the obligation to his mother, the choice he made that violated it. He does not resolve the contradiction. He just holds it.

Then the journalist asks about a specific operational detail: the journalist has learned that Pakistani army provided cover fire for the infiltrating group during the border crossing. Is this true?

The terrorist corrects this. The Pakistani army did not provide cover fire directly. What they provided was resources — weapons, training, logistical support. The cover fire during the crossing came from their own men — militants on the Pakistani side of the border. The Pakistani army equipped and trained them but maintained a degree of separation from the actual crossing. Their people did the cover fire. Pakistani military provided everything else.

**What this scene does:** It closes his interview arc with two revelations simultaneously. The personal one: he has a mother who doesn't know where he is, who he was, what he did. He sends her money. He knows he should have served her. He chose something else. The operational one: the Pakistani military's involvement was real, substantial, and deliberately structured to maintain deniability.

**Design implication — The captured terrorist as a character on the website:** He should not be presented as a villain and he should not be presented sympathetically. He should be presented as what he is: a young man who made catastrophic choices, who is sitting with the consequences, who has a mother who doesn't know, who holds contradictions without resolving them. Human. Complicated. Real.

---

### SCENE 30 — THE FINAL OPERATION. FULL CIRCLE. THE FILM ENDS WHERE IT BEGAN.

Night. A police vehicle moving through Kashmir. Officers communicating with teams. A situation developing.

This is the same operation from Scene 1 — the night patrol, the confrontation, the darkness. The film has now given us everything we needed to understand what we were watching at the beginning. We know now what these soldiers are, what they are doing, why they are here, what it costs. We know the families. We know the captured militant. We know the fathers. We know the girls on cycles and the mothers asking for their sons.

The situation intensifies. A real operation. Against a militant group — specifically identified as Hizbul Mujahideen — who are in the cordon. This group is responsible for the deaths of four to five police officers.

The operation is live. The footage is raw. Real firing. Real coordination. Real danger.

And then: the operation ends. The militants are dead. The same top-view shot as before — bodies on the ground, seen from above.

The film has come full circle. Scene 1 and Scene 30 are the same operation. Now we understand it completely.

**What this scene does:** The circular structure is the film's formal statement about the conflict. It goes on. The operations happen at night. The soldiers go out. Sometimes they come back. The militants cross. Sometimes they are caught. Sometimes they are killed. And in the morning Kashmir is beautiful and somewhere a father is sitting with his loss and somewhere a mother is asking for her son and the cycle continues.

---

### SCENE 31 — THE CLOSING. FACES. SILENCE. GRIEF.

The film's final sequence.

No narration. No new information. No more interviews.

Just faces.

One by one, the film shows the faces of the people who appeared in it. Every interview subject. The grieving fathers. The daughters. The officers. The captured militant. The girls. Every person who sat across from the journalist and spoke.

Each face held for a moment. Music underneath — instrumental, mournful, quiet. Not dramatic. Just present.

And then the face is gone and the next face appears.

**What this scene does:** It refuses to let the film end with analysis, or conclusions, or calls to action, or political statements. It ends with people. The specific, individual, irreplaceable people who are living inside this conflict. Their faces are not illustrating a point. They are the point.

**Design implication — The website's closing element:** The site should have a closing section that mirrors this. Not a footer with links. Not a call to action. Just the faces. A gallery of the people in this film, held in the same quiet dignity as the film's final sequence. The user should be able to scroll through them the way the film shows them — one at a time, or together — and feel what the film's ending makes you feel: that these are real people. That this is happening to real people. That the conflict is not an abstraction.

## PART THREE — THE FILM'S EMOTIONAL ARCHITECTURE
### The Shape of What the Viewer Experiences

---

Understanding this arc is essential for the website's pacing and structure.

**The film's emotional arc:**

1. **Disorientation** — thrown into darkness and gunfire with no context (Scene 1)
2. **Beauty** — a deliberate exhale into Kashmir's visual splendor (Scene 2)
3. **Information** — establishing the facts, the evidence, the framework (Scenes 3-7)
4. **First grief** — the funeral, the family, the specific human cost (Scene 8)
5. **Analysis deepens** — the IPS officer, the mechanism of radicalization (Scene 9)
6. **The father interviews** — the film's heart, the deepest grief (Scenes 10-11)
7. **Violence reminder** — the confrontation footage, the dead (Scene 13)
8. **The moral argument** — the officer who counts saved, not killed (Scene 14)
9. **Grace** — the surrender, the parents calling out (Scene 16)
10. **Breath** — the girls on cycles, the ordinary (Scene 17)
11. **More grief** — return to the funeral, the father (Scene 18)
12. **Deepening analysis** — the drug-terror nexus, the ecosystem (Scene 26)
13. **Maximum grief** — the three consecutive interviews, the mother asking (Scene 27)
14. **Ideology dismantled** — the terrorist's collapse (Scene 25)
15. **Full circle** — the final operation (Scene 30)
16. **Silence** — the faces (Scene 31)

**The website's pacing should follow this arc.** Not bullet points of information. A journey that moves from disorientation to beauty to grief to analysis to grace to deeper grief to silence. The user should feel, by the end of the site, something close to what a viewer feels at the end of the film.

---

## PART FOUR — THE FILM'S CENTRAL DUALITY
### The One Truth That Everything Else Orbits

---

Kashmir — Fighting for Peace holds one truth above all others, and everything in the film returns to it:

> **Kashmir is one of the most beautiful places on earth. And it is quietly, persistently, generationally bleeding. Both of these things are true at the same time. Neither cancels the other out.**

The beauty does not make the grief easier. The grief does not make the beauty less real. They exist together, in permanent, unresolved coexistence, in the same mountains, the same streets, the same morning light, the same families.

This is not a poetic device. It is the lived reality of everyone in this film. The soldiers patrolling at night are also people who have seen those mountains in the morning. The fathers who have lost sons to militancy also live in a place of extraordinary beauty that their sons grew up in. The girls cycling want to live their ordinary lives in a place that is also, genuinely, one of the most beautiful on earth.

**This duality is the design brief.** Every visual choice, every color decision, every animation, every layout — must carry this coexistence of beauty and grief. Not alternating between them. Both. Simultaneously.

## PART FIVE — DESIGN SYSTEM
### Every Decision Derived From The Film

---

### Color Palette

| Name | Hex | Role | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Midnight Kashmir | `#0A0C0F` | Primary background | The darkness of night operations, the weight of grief — near-black with a cold blue undertone like Kashmir pre-dawn |
| Deep Slate | `#111418` | Surface / panels | Slightly lifted from base — cards, interview panels, content areas |
| Saffron Ember | `#C97B2B` | Primary accent | Kashmir saffron — warmth surviving inside darkness. Used for key headlines, hover states, the beauty side of the duality |
| Snow White | `#E8EDF2` | Body text | Clean, readable, cold-white like Kashmir snow on a clear day |
| Ash | `#3A3F47` | Borders / dividers | The quiet structural elements — never decorative, always functional |
| Dull Crimson | `#7A1F1F` | Loss / conflict marker | Used sparingly — perhaps three or four times on the entire site. Never as decoration. Only where loss is being specifically acknowledged. Its rarity is its power. |
| Muted Sage | `#4A5C4A` | Nature / terrain tie | For landscape-connected elements — the green of Kashmir's valleys, subdued |
| Film Grain White | `#FFFFFF` at 4% opacity | Grain texture | The noise overlay that ties the digital to the documentary's raw footage |

**Color rule:** This site is almost entirely dark. Saffron Ember and Snow White do all the work of light. Crimson appears so rarely it stops the viewer when it appears. If you are reaching for a bright color for visual interest — stop. The interest comes from content, not decoration.

### Typography

**Display — Playfair Display (serif)**
- Used for: film title, major section headers, pull quotes of maximum emotional weight
- Treatment: large, tracked in all-caps for power statements; large italic for emotional moments
- Why: carries the gravity and age of a place with centuries of history and culture; the serif acknowledges the artisanal culture the film documents

**Body — DM Sans**
- Used for: all body copy, interview excerpts, descriptive text, navigation
- Treatment: clean, journalistic, direct — no decorative font choices in the reading layer
- Why: journalistic clarity; the film is journalism and the reading experience should feel like serious, respectful reporting

**Utility — Space Mono (monospace)**
- Used for: scene labels, timestamps, military codes, technical overlays, caption metadata
- Treatment: small, tracked, all-caps
- Why: the radio codes of Scene 1 (delta, MGL, position numbers) — this typeface carries the raw dispatch energy of field reporting; it also distinguishes operational/contextual information from emotional content

**Type scale:**
- Hero title: 96–120px Playfair Display, tracked
- Section headers: 48–64px Playfair Display
- Pull quotes: 32–40px Playfair Display italic
- Body: 16–18px DM Sans, 1.7 line height
- Captions / labels: 11–13px Space Mono, tracked, all-caps

### Motion Philosophy

**The governing principle:** Motion in this site serves the film's rhythm, not the designer's portfolio.

The film moves slowly between heavy things. It does not jump or bounce or delight. When it transitions, it cuts or fades — directly, without flourish. The site's motion must mirror this.

**Specific motion rules:**

- **Reveals:** Content emerges from darkness slowly — not snapping in. Like developing film in a darkroom. Fade from black, not slide from off-screen.
- **Parallax:** Landscape images move slower than overlaid text on scroll. The viewer is looking through a window at Kashmir. The window moves with them. Kashmir stays.
- **Interview quote entrance:** Key quotes in the interview sections appear word by word, or line by line — the pacing of testimony being spoken aloud. Slow. Deliberate. Not a loading effect. An arrival.
- **Film grain:** A subtle animated noise texture overlays the entire site at 4–7% opacity. The viewer may not consciously notice it. Its absence would make the site feel too clean, too finished, too safe. The grain says: this is documentary. This is real.
- **Hover on portraits:** Portrait images in the Voices section are dark and desaturated. On hover, they lift very slightly in brightness — not fully, not warmly. Just enough to acknowledge the viewer's attention.
- **Cursor:** Custom cursor — small white dot with a larger semi-transparent ring. Ring scales on hover over interactive elements. Over portrait faces, an optional label appears: a single word that captures the person's role — FATHER. OFFICER. SOLDIER. DAUGHTER.
- **Section transitions:** Sections do not share a visual border. They emerge from black. The transition between sections is darkness — brief, like a cut between scenes in the film.
- **The Duality section:** Scroll-driven split experience. As the user scrolls, the left panel shows Kashmir's beauty (saffron-warm, landscape stills); the right panel shows the corresponding grief (dark, a quote, a loss). They are synchronized. The user controls the speed of the revelation. This is the site's signature interaction.

**What motion must never do:**
- Bounce, spring, or ease in a way that reads as playful
- Call attention to itself
- Distract from the content it is revealing
- Make the site feel like a showcase rather than a memorial

### Texture and Atmosphere

- **Film grain overlay:** CSS-based animated noise, full viewport, 4–7% white opacity. Always present. Never distracting.
- **Image treatment:** All photographs and stills should be dark-graded — not heavily processed, but with a slight reduction in highlights and a cool-to-neutral color shift. The beauty of Kashmir should still be visible and real, but viewed as if through the lens of everything we know about it.
- **No gradients that feel designed:** Any gradient should feel like light naturally falling, not a CSS effect. Dark-to-darker transitions. Light only where the content earns it.
- **Portrait treatment:** Interview subject portraits — dark background, high contrast, desaturated. They should look like documentary photography, not press headshots.

## PART SIX — PAGE ARCHITECTURE
### Every Section, In Order, With Full Purpose

---

### Section 1 — HERO

**Purpose:** Mirror the film's opening. Drop the user into something real.

**What it contains:**
- Full viewport. Dark. Almost nothing.
- A cinematic still or very slow looping clip — Kashmir pre-dawn, or a silhouette, or mountains in mist. Dark-graded.
- Silence for a beat — 1.5–2 seconds — before anything appears.
- Then the title emerges: **KASHMIR** — letter by letter, Playfair Display, large, tracked, white
- Then: **— FIGHTING FOR PEACE** — slightly smaller, slightly delayed
- Then: **A Film by Rig 360 Media** — Space Mono, small, tracked, below
- Then a single line — a quote from or about the film — in Playfair italic. Something that contains the central contradiction. Example register (not exact wording): *Two truths. Same sky. Same soil.*
- A single CTA below — minimal, reluctant: **WATCH** or **ENTER THE FILM** — DM Sans, small caps, with a subtle underline animation on hover
- Film grain present from the first frame
- No navbar visible on hero — or if present, completely transparent

**Emotional register:** The user should feel like they are being let into something, not invited into something. The darkness before the title appears should have weight.

---

### Section 2 — THE FILM

**Purpose:** Tell the viewer what this film is, honestly, without PR language.

**What it contains:**
- Two-column layout: left — a landscape image of Kashmir (parallax on scroll, slightly slow); right — a serious, journalistic description of the documentary
- The description is honest. Not superlatives. Not awards language. Something like: *This film went to Kashmir. It asked people to speak. It listened to what they said.* Then what they said, at a high level.
- One large pull quote, centered below the two columns — something that captures the film's moral weight. It should be the kind of sentence that makes someone stop scrolling.

**Emotional register:** The viewer is being oriented. This should feel like the film's opening narration — grounding, serious, respectful.

---

### Section 3 — THE DUALITY (SIGNATURE ELEMENT)

**Purpose:** Make the user experience the film's central truth, not just read about it.

**What it contains:**
- Full viewport, scroll-driven
- Split screen: left half, right half
- As the user scrolls, paired moments reveal themselves:
  - **Beauty:** Kashmir morning mist over mountains — **Grief:** A father describing his last phone call with his son
  - **Beauty:** A craftsman's hands working wood — **Grief:** A funeral ceremony
  - **Beauty:** Snow on the valley — **Grief:** The mother who keeps asking for her son
  - **Beauty:** Girls on cycles in sunlight — **Grief:** The Sikh officer's daughter describing how he was targeted
  - **Beauty:** Kashmir's streets at dawn — **Grief:** A body seen from above
- The left side is warmer — saffron-touched, natural
- The right side is darker — near-monochrome, text-heavy
- They are always the same Kashmir
- A thin line between them — Ash color — that holds them together without separating them

**Emotional register:** This should feel like the film itself. The user controls the scroll, which means the user controls the pace of revelation. They cannot skip the grief by scrolling past the beauty. They are always looking at both simultaneously.

---

### Section 4 — THE VOICES

**Purpose:** Introduce the film's interview subjects with the dignity the film gave them.

**What it contains:**
- A grid of portraits — dark, high-contrast, documentary style
- Subjects include: the captured Pakistani militant, the grieving fathers, the police officers, the army officers, the IPS officer, the Sikh officer's daughter, the young Kashmiri girls, the fallen officer's father
- Labels in Space Mono: not full names unless public — **FATHER, SOUTH KASHMIR / ARMY OFFICER, LOC POSTING / CAPTURED MILITANT / DAUGHTER** etc.
- Default state: dark, desaturated, quiet
- Hover state: slight brightness lift, a quote from that person appears over the image in Playfair italic — their words, or a close paraphrase of what they said
- Click/tap: expands to a panel — their portrait larger, their role, a longer passage of what they shared in the film

**Emotional register:** The viewer is meeting the film's people. The same refusal of easy categorization the film practices — every person presented with equal dignity regardless of which "side" their story falls on.

---

### Section 5 — THE STORY

**Purpose:** Let the viewer move through the film's narrative arc.

**What it contains:**
- A horizontal scroll timeline or vertical chapter structure
- 12 chapters corresponding to the film's acts (see Part Two)
- Each chapter: a dark card with a scene label in Space Mono (**ACT 01 — NIGHT**), a single evocative line describing the chapter, and a dark-graded still
- The chapters are in order — the film's actual sequence
- On hover: the card lifts slightly, the image brightens very slightly
- This is an invitation into the film's structure, not a plot summary — the language should make someone want to watch, not feel like they've already seen it

**Emotional register:** Navigational but not clinical. The viewer is being walked through the film's shape.

---

### Section 6 — THE THEMES

**Purpose:** Name the film's analytical dimensions without reducing them.

**What it contains:**
Four thematic pillars, presented as dark minimal cards:

1. **PROXY WAR** — How a neighboring state uses a population's own children as instruments of violence against them
2. **THE FAMILY IN THE MIDDLE** — What happens to the people who loved someone who was taken by the conflict — from either direction
3. **THE BURDEN OF SERVICE** — What it means to be a soldier or officer who wants to save rather than kill, in a conflict that keeps producing reasons for both
4. **FAITH CORRUPTED** — How religious identity is weaponized for recruitment, and how the people of that faith refuse to let it be defined that way

Each card: dark background, a small icon (simple, not decorative), the theme name in Space Mono, a two-sentence description in DM Sans. No flourish. The weight comes from the content.

---

### Section 7 — WATCH

**Purpose:** Give the viewer access to the film.

**What it contains:**
- If a trailer exists: full-width embed, dark frame, no autoplay, a single play button
- If the full film is available: access information
- If neither is publicly available yet: a minimal holding state — dark, simple, honest. **The film is coming. No countdown clock. No hype. Just: it is being prepared for you.**

**Emotional register:** After everything the site has shown the viewer, the CTA to watch should feel earned, not sold.

---

### Section 8 — THE CLOSING GALLERY

**Purpose:** Mirror the film's ending. End with people, not with calls to action.

**What it contains:**
- Each interview subject's portrait, shown one by one in a slow scroll or automatic sequence
- No narration text. No descriptions. Just the faces.
- The instrumental music from the film's closing, if available — or silence — or a single ambient tone
- After the last face: a single line in Playfair italic. Something about bearing witness. Something quiet.
- Then: the production credit. **Kashmir — Fighting for Peace. A Film by Rig 360 Media.**
- Then: the footer.

**Emotional register:** The user should feel, at the end of the site, what a viewer feels at the end of the film. That these are real people. That this is happening to real people. That the story does not end when the credits roll.

---

### Footer

**Contents:**
- Film title and year
- Production: Rig 360 Media
- Press / contact link if applicable
- Nothing else. No social media grid. No newsletter signup. Nothing that breaks the register.
- Optional: one final small line of text, centered, in Playfair italic — something from the film that belongs at the end.

## PART SEVEN — TECHNICAL SPECIFICATIONS

---

**Framework:** React / Next.js (confirm with backend teammate before proceeding)

**Animation:**
- GSAP + ScrollTrigger for all scroll-driven animations (the Duality section, section reveals, the Story timeline)
- Framer Motion for component-level transitions (portrait expand, card hover states)
- Lenis for smooth scroll inertia

**Film Grain:**
- CSS-based preferred for performance — animated SVG filter or CSS noise at low opacity
- Canvas fallback if CSS grain is insufficient on target browsers

**Fonts:**
- Google Fonts: Playfair Display, DM Sans, Space Mono
- Load only the weights actually used — no full family imports

**Images:**
- All images lazy-loaded
- Blur-up placeholder on load (low-res base64 placeholder → full image)
- Video: poster frame displayed before load, no autoplay, user-initiated only

**CSS Architecture:**
- CSS custom properties for every token — no hardcoded color or type value anywhere in the codebase
- Component-scoped CSS — no global class conflicts
- Mobile-first responsive — the emotional weight must survive on a phone screen

**Accessibility:**
- `prefers-reduced-motion` respected — all scroll animations disabled, fade-ins minimal
- Keyboard navigable throughout
- Sufficient contrast on all text (WCAG AA minimum, AAA where possible given the dark palette)
- Alt text for all images — specific, descriptive, honoring the subject

**Performance:**
- Core Web Vitals targets: LCP < 2.5s, CLS < 0.1, FID < 100ms
- No blocking third-party scripts
- Font loading: `font-display: swap`

**Dark mode:** This site has no light mode. The documentary does not have a light mode.

## PART EIGHT — WHAT THIS SITE IS NOT

Read this and let it shape every decision.

❌ **Not a news site.** This is not reporting on Kashmir. This is an invitation into a film about Kashmir. The register is cinematic, not journalistic — even though the film itself is journalism.

❌ **Not a political site.** The film does not argue for a political position. The site must not either. No language that takes sides. No visual language associated with either nation's political identity.

❌ **Not a war movie site.** No weapons as decoration. No military iconography used for visual interest. The conflict is documented, not celebrated.

❌ **Not a grief tourism site.** The grief in this film belongs to specific people. The site must not use that grief as aesthetic material — as "dark and beautiful" design inspiration. The darkness must be earned by the content, not applied as a vibe.

❌ **Not a template.** There are three generic "serious documentary" looks that AI-generated design defaults to. This site should not look like any of them. It should look like it could only be the site for *this film* about *these people* in *this place*.

❌ **Not finished-feeling.** The conflict in this film is not resolved. The film does not end with resolution. The site should not feel resolved either. It should feel like something ongoing — something the viewer is now part of, in a small way, by having been here.

---

## PART NINE — THE TEST

Before publishing anything — before committing any section, any animation, any copy choice — apply this test:

**Does this honor the specific humans in this film?**

**Not: does this look like a serious documentary website.**
**Not: is this dark and cinematic enough.**
**Not: is this technically impressive.**

**Does this honor the father who accepted his son's death? The mother who keeps asking where her son is? The girls who just want to ride bikes? The officer who wants to count saved, not killed? The daughter who lost her father because someone looked at a row of buses and chose his?**

If yes — build it.

If it just looks right but doesn't feel earned — go back. Find what is thin. Make it true.

The bar is not aesthetic. The bar is human.
